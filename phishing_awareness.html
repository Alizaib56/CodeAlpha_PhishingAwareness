Module 01

How Phishing Works

Phishing attacks follow a predictable pattern. Recognizing each stage makes them far easier to spot before any damage is done.

01

Attacker Crafts the Lure

A fake email, SMS, or webpage is created to closely mimic a trusted source — your bank, employer, or a popular service like Google or PayPal.

02

Message is Delivered

The phishing message is sent via email, WhatsApp, SMS, or social media DMs — often to thousands of people simultaneously.

03

Urgency is Created

The message uses fear: "Your account will be suspended," "Verify now," or "Claim your prize before it expires!" — to stop you thinking clearly.

04

Victim Clicks the Link

The victim clicks the link and lands on a convincing fake page — a cloned login screen or payment form — and enters their credentials.

05

Data is Harvested

The attacker instantly captures whatever was entered — username, password, credit card number, or OTP — and uses it within minutes.

06

Damage is Done

Accounts are taken over, money is transferred, or stolen credentials are sold on the dark web. Most victims don't realize until it's too late.

Module 02

Recognize a Phishing Email

Every phishing email contains telltale signs. Here's a real-world example with annotated red flags — learn to spot them instantly.

From:support@paypa1-security.com ①
To:you@email.com
Subject:⚠ Urgent: Your account has been compromised ②

Dear Valued Customer ③,

We have detected suspicious activity on your PayPal account. Your account will be permanently suspended within 24 hours ④ if you do not verify your information immediately.

To restore full access, click the secure link below:

🔒 Verify My Account Now → http://paypa1-login.xyz/verify ⑤

Regards,
PayPal Security Team ⑥

①

Fake sender domain — "paypa1-security.com" uses the number "1" instead of "l". Real PayPal only sends from @paypal.com.

②

Alarming subject line — Designed to trigger panic so you open without thinking critically.

③

Generic greeting — Legitimate companies address you by your real name, not "Valued Customer."

④

Artificial urgency — The 24-hour deadline prevents you from verifying the email's authenticity.

⑤

Suspicious URL — "paypa1-login.xyz" is not PayPal's domain. Never click links in emails.

⑥

No contact info — Real companies include official helpline numbers and verified support links.

Module 03

Spotting Fake Websites

Fake sites are designed to look identical to the real thing. Always inspect the URL bar carefully before entering any information.

🔒https://www.paypal.com/login

Legitimate Website

The domain is exactly "paypal.com" — nothing before it, HTTPS is present, and the lock icon is valid. This is what a genuine URL looks like.

✓ SAFE
⚠http://paypa1-login.xyz/secure

Typosquat Attack

The number "1" replaces the letter "l" in "paypal." Uses HTTP instead of HTTPS. The domain is completely unrelated to PayPal.

✗ FAKE — DO NOT ENTER
🔒paypal.com.secure-verify.net/login

Subdomain Trick

"paypal.com" appears in the URL but as a subdomain of "secure-verify.net" — which is the actual malicious domain. The lock icon can still appear.

✗ FAKE — DO NOT ENTER
🔒paypa|.com/login

Homoglyph Attack

The letter "l" is replaced with a visually identical Unicode character "|". The URL looks perfectly real at a glance — always look very carefully.

✗ FAKE — DO NOT ENTER
🔒https://www.google.com/search

Check These 3 Things

① Is HTTPS present? ② Is the domain exactly correct with no extra words? ③ Does it match what you searched for? All three must be yes.

✓ VERIFY ALWAYS
🛡GOLDEN RULE

Never Click — Always Type

Instead of clicking links in emails or messages, always type the official website address directly into your browser. This eliminates every URL trick above.

✓ BEST PRACTICE
Module 04

Social Engineering Tactics

Attackers exploit human psychology, not just technology. Understanding these tactics is your best defense against manipulation.

CRITICAL
⏱

Urgency & Scarcity

"Act within 24 hours or your account is deleted." Time pressure prevents you from pausing to think or verify the request independently.

CRITICAL
😨

Fear & Threats

"Your device has been hacked" or "Police have flagged your account." Fear clouds judgment and makes victims act impulsively without verification.

HIGH RISK
🏆

Greed & Rewards

"You've won PKR 50,000!" Promises of prizes, refunds, or easy money attract victims who don't stop to question why they were randomly selected.

HIGH RISK
🎭

Authority Impersonation

Attacker pretends to be your bank, FIA, CEO, or tech support. People are conditioned to comply with authority figures without questioning them.

COMMON
❤️

Trust & Familiarity

"Hi, it's your cousin — I lost my phone." Attackers research victims on social media to use real names, locations, or relationships to seem genuine.

COMMON
🤝

Reciprocity

"We're giving you a free upgrade — just verify your account to claim it." A perceived gift lowers your guard and makes you feel obligated to comply.

Module 05

Best Practices to Stay Safe

These habits, applied consistently, make you significantly harder to phish. Share them with everyone you know.

🔗

Hover Before You Click

Hover over any link to preview its actual destination in your browser's status bar. Suspicious? Don't click.

🔐

Enable 2FA Everywhere

Two-factor authentication means even if your password is stolen, attackers can't log in without the second step.

📧

Verify Senders Independently

If your bank emails you, don't click the link — call their official number on the back of your card to verify.

🚫

Never Share OTPs

No legitimate organization — bank, telecom, or government — will ever ask for your OTP or password over any channel.

🛡

Keep Software Updated

Updates patch the vulnerabilities that phishing sites and malware exploit. Install them promptly on all devices.

📱

Use Phishing Filters

Enable anti-phishing filters in your email client and browser. Report phishing emails rather than just deleting them.

🌐

Check HTTPS + Domain

Before entering info on any site, confirm the padlock is present and the domain name is exactly correct — no extra words.

🧠

Pause When Rushed

Urgency is a manipulation tactic. If a message pressures immediate action — stop, breathe, and verify through official channels.

Module 06

Real-World Phishing Examples

These attack scenarios are based on real phishing campaigns that have targeted ordinary users. Each one is a lesson.

Email Phishing

A cloned email from "Netflix" tells a user their payment failed and they must update card details to avoid cancellation. The link leads to a perfect replica — on a different domain.

What happened: The victim entered their card details. Within hours, unauthorized transactions were made internationally.

  • Sender was "noreply@netflix-billing.info" not "@netflix.com"
  • URL was "netflix-update-payment.com" not "netflix.com"
  • Email used generic "Hi there" greeting
  • Extreme urgency — "update now or lose access today"
noreply@netflix-billing.info
Your payment of PKR 1,200 was declined. Update your payment method within 24 hours or your account will be suspended.

→ http://netflix-update-payment.com/billing
✓ Netflix only sends emails from @netflix.com. Always go to netflix.com directly from your browser — never via a link in email.

SMS Phishing (Smishing)

A text message appears to be from a courier: "Your parcel could not be delivered. Pay PKR 50 customs fee to release it." A link leads to a fake payment page.

Why it works: People are often expecting parcels. The small fee seems low-risk — but card details entered are stolen for far larger charges.

  • You weren't expecting any parcel delivery
  • URL is a short link or random domain
  • The small "fee" creates false legitimacy
  • No official tracking number or reference provided
TCS-Pakistan (Fake)
Your parcel (ID: TCS-44821) could not be delivered. A customs fee of PKR 50 is required. Pay now: bit.ly/tcs-delivery-pay
✓ Legitimate couriers charge fees at the door, not via SMS links. Call TCS directly on their official number to verify any delivery fees.

Voice Phishing (Vishing)

A caller claims to be from "HBL Fraud Prevention." They say suspicious transactions were detected and ask the victim to confirm their card number, CVV, and OTP.

The tactic: The caller sounds professional, uses your real name, and creates fear of losing money if you don't comply immediately.

  • Banks don't call asking for card details or OTPs
  • Caller knows your name but asks for all account info
  • They request your OTP to "stop the fraudulent transfer"
  • Heavy urgency — "we must act in the next 2 minutes"
Caller: "HBL Fraud Department"
"Sir, we've detected a transaction of PKR 45,000. To reverse it immediately, please confirm your card number and share the OTP we just sent you."
✓ Hang up immediately. Call HBL directly on 111-111-425. Banks NEVER ask for your OTP, card number, or CVV over the phone.

Spear Phishing (Targeted)

An employee receives an email appearing to be from their CEO: "I'm in a meeting — please urgently transfer PKR 200,000 to this vendor account." This is Business Email Compromise (BEC).

Why it's dangerous: The attacker researched the org chart on LinkedIn and spoofed the CEO's email address convincingly.

  • Email asks to bypass normal approval processes
  • Unusual urgency from a senior person
  • Reply-to address differs from the display address
  • Demand for secrecy is a major red flag
CEO Ahmed Khan <ahmed.khan@company-mail.net>
"Please process an urgent wire transfer of PKR 200,000 to vendor account below. I'm in a board meeting — do NOT discuss with anyone else."
✓ Always verify financial requests via a separate confirmed channel — call the CEO on their known number. Secrecy demands are always a red flag.
